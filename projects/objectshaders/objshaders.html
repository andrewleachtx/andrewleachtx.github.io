    Rasterization

    Almost every 3D game or application we've played was using rasterization behind the scenes.

    If a raster is a group of pixels on a 2D grid or image, then rasterization is the process by which we convert 3D shapes (composed of triangles) into pixels on your 2D screen.

    Why compose objects of triangles though?

    The Cake Is a Lie

    The cake is a lie. Everything you are looking at is composed of triangles. Take a look at Minecraft for example. A cube is 6 squares or faces, and one of those squares is two triangles.

    Even for more complex shapes, like a sphere, we can approximate it with a bunch of triangles. The degree of precision necessary to represent a mesh can then be described as low-poly or high-poly.

    Tesla Cybertruck (low-poly)

    Triangles require the least amount of points or coordinates to enclose a 2D area, which makes them very simple. Also, they lie on a single plane - making them play nice with math. And the GPU does a lot of math. We want to minimize the overhead and complexity of the calculations we do, to increase performance.

    Of course, that is to say performance is usually the goal, at least for real-time rendering in video games or other contexts. Now, if creating perfect images and scenes (prerendered) is your goal, ray tracing is your friend.
